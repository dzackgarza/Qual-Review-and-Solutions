The Symmetric Group

  • A cycle is even ⇔ product of an even number of transpositions.

  • A cycle of even length is odd

  • A cycle of odd length is even

Mnemonic: the parity of a $k{\hbox{-}}$cycle is the parity of k − 1.

The alternating group is the subgroup of even permutations, i.e.
An ≔ {σ∈Sn | sign(σ)=1}
where sign(σ) = ( − 1)m and m is the number of cycles of even length.

Every σ ∈ An has an even number of odd cycles (i.e. an even number of even-length cycles).


$$ A_4 = & \{\text{id}, \\ & (1,3)(2,4), (1,2)(3,4), (1,4)(2,3), \\ & (1,2,3), (1,3,2), \\ & (1,2,4), (1,4,2), \\ & (1,3,4), (1,4,3), \\ & (2,3,4), (2,4,3) \} $$

A subgroup of Sn is transitive iff its action on {1,2,⋯,n} is transitive.

Useful Facts:

  • σ ∘ (a1⋯ak) ∘ σ − 1 = (σ(a1), ⋯σ(ak))
  • Conjugacy classes are determined by cycle type
  • The order of a cycle is its length.
  • The order of an element is the least common multiple of the sizes of its cycles.
  • An ≥ 5 is simple.

Counting Theorems


$$H \leq G \implies {\left\lvert {H} \right\rvert} {~\Bigm|~}{\left\lvert {G} \right\rvert}.$$

The order of every element divides the size of G, i.e.
$$ g\in G \implies o(g) {~\Bigm|~}o(G) \implies g^{{\left\lvert {G} \right\rvert}} = e .$$

There does not necessarily exist H ≤ G with |H| = n for every $n {~\Bigm|~}{\left\lvert {G} \right\rvert}$. Counterexample: |A4| = 12 but has no subgroup of order 6.

For every prime p dividing |G|. there is an element (and thus a subgroup) of order p.

This is a partial converse to Lagrange’s theorem, and strengthened by Sylow’s theorem.

Group Actions

An action of G on X is a group morphism
$$ \phi:G \times X &\rightarrow X \\ (g,x) &\mapsto g\cdot x $$
or equivalently
$$ \phi: G &\longrightarrow{\mathrm{Aut}}(X) \\ g \mapsto (x \mapsto \phi_g (x) \coloneqq g\cdot x) $$
satisfying

  1. e ⋅ x = x
  2. g ⋅ (h ⋅ x) = (gh) ⋅ x

Useful fact: ker ψ = ⋂x ∈ XGx is the intersection of all stabilizers.

A group action G ↷ X is transitive iff for all x, y ∈ X there exists a g ∈ G such that g ⋅ x = x. Equivalently, the action has a single orbit.

Reminder of notation: for a group G acting on a set X,

  • G ⋅ x = {g⋅x | g∈G} ⊆ X is the orbit

  • Gx = {g∈G | g⋅x=x} ⊆ G is the stabilizer

  • X/G ⊂ 2X is the set of orbits

  • Xg = {x∈X | g⋅x=x} ⊆ X are the fixed points

Note that being in the same orbit is an equivalence relation which partitions X, and G acts transitively if restricted to any single orbit.


|G⋅x| = [G : Gx] = |G|/|Gx|  if G is finite

Mnemonic: G/Gx ≅ G ⋅ x.

Examples of Orbit-Stabilizer

  1. Let G act on itself by left translation, where g ↦ (h ↦ gh).

  • The orbit G ⋅ x = G is the entire group

  • The stabilizer Gx is only the identity.

  • The fixed points Xg are only the identity.

  1. Let G act on itself by conjugation.

  • G ⋅ x is the conjugacy class of x (so not generally transitive)

  • Gx = Z(x) ≔ CG(x) = {g | [g,x]=e}, the centralizer of x.

  • Gg (the fixed points) is the center Z(G).

The number of conjugates of an element (i.e. the size of its conjugacy class) is the index of its centralizer, [G : CG(x)].

The Class Equation


$$ {\left\lvert {G} \right\rvert} = {\left\lvert {Z(G)} \right\rvert} + \sum_{\substack{\text{One $x_i$ from} \\ \text{each conjugacy} \\ \text{class}}} [G: C_G(x_i)] $$

Note that [G : CG(xi)] is the number of elements in the conjugacy class of xi, and each xi ∈ Z(G) has a singleton conjugacy class.

Examples

  1. Let G act on X, its set of subgroups, by conjugation.

  • G ⋅ H = {gHg − 1} is the set of conjugate subgroups of H

  • GH = NG(H) is the normalizer of in G of H

  • Xg is the set of normal subgroups of G

Given H ≤ G, the number of conjugate subgroups is [G : NG(H)].

  1. For a fixed proper subgroup H < G, let G act on its cosets G/H = {gH | g∈G} by left translation.

  • G ⋅ gH = G/H, i.e. this is a transitive action.

  • GgH = gHg − 1 is a conjugate subgroup of H

  • (G/H)G = ∅

If G is simple, H < G proper, and [G : H] = n, then there exists an injective map ϕ : G ↪ Sn.

This action induces ϕ; it is nontrivial since gH = H for all g implies H = G; ker ϕ ⊴ G and G simple implies ker ϕ = 1.

Slogan: the number of orbits is equal to the average number of fixed points, i.e.
$$ {\left\lvert {X/G} \right\rvert} = \frac{1}{{\left\lvert {G} \right\rvert} }\sum_{g\in G} {\left\lvert {X^g} \right\rvert} $$
